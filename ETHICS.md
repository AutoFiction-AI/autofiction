# Ethics

## Position

Autofiction is released as a research artifact for transparency, scrutiny, and reproducibility in long-form AI writing. The project is not intended to normalize deceptive publishing practices or to obscure machine authorship behind human branding.

Our position is straightforward:

- novels generated wholly or substantially with this pipeline should be clearly disclosed as AI-generated when distributed publicly
- undisclosed AI publishing is unethical
- misrepresenting machine-generated books as solely human-authored is deceptive to readers, editors, publishers, and reviewers
- the pipeline should be used in ways that preserve provenance rather than erase it

All novels published on [autofiction.ai](https://autofiction.ai) are clearly labeled as AI-generated. The platform exists to study how readers engage with AI-written fiction under transparent conditions, not to pass machine-generated work off as human-written.

## Disclosure Expectations

When publishing or distributing outputs generated with this system, responsible use includes clear disclosure of:

- the fact that AI systems generated substantial portions of the text
- the role of human intervention, if any, in editing, selection, or revision
- the existence of provenance artifacts or generation records when those records are available

Exact disclosure wording will vary by venue, but the underlying requirement should not: readers should not have to guess whether a book was machine-generated.

## Why We Are Releasing This

This repository is released to support:

- research on long-form generation pipelines
- critical inspection of prompt-and-revision methods
- reproducibility of reported outputs and behaviors
- debate about the limits and failure modes of AI-generated fiction

It is not being released as a template for covertly flooding markets, contests, journals, or recommendation systems with undisclosed AI books.

## Commercialization And Outputs

The Apache 2.0 software license for this repository permits broad reuse of the code, including commercial reuse. That legal fact does not change our ethical position.

We do not endorse commercial publication of generated outputs without explicit disclosure, careful rights review, and compliance with the policies of the platform or venue involved.

If you generate your own outputs with this pipeline, you are responsible for:

- licensing those outputs separately if you want usage restrictions on them
- complying with venue disclosure rules and applicable law
- ensuring that publication claims about authorship are truthful

The models used by this pipeline were trained on corpora that include human-authored creative work. We acknowledge the ongoing debate about the implications of such training but do not take a position on it here. What we do insist on is that the outputs be disclosed rather than disguised.

The generated books and artifact corpus are published under [CC BY-NC 4.0](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-nc/4.0/), separate from the Apache 2.0 license on this codebase.

## Unacceptable Uses

Examples of unacceptable use include:

- submitting generated books to publishers, journals, prizes, or retailers in violation of disclosure requirements
- presenting generated work as solely human-authored when it was not
- fabricating provenance, editing history, or review history to disguise machine authorship
- stripping or removing provenance metadata, generation logs, or other records from outputs in order to obscure machine authorship
- using the system to impersonate living authors or to mislead readers about source or sponsorship
- using generated content in ways that violate privacy, publicity, copyright, or other legal rights

## Keeping Records

Where possible, keep and share the records that make machine authorship inspectable: prompts, review artifacts, revision reports, and other structured evidence. Strong provenance is not perfect protection against misuse, but it is far better than asking readers to trust unsupported claims.

## Impact On Human Authors

AI-generated fiction raises legitimate concerns about its impact on working writers and the creative economy. Automated systems that produce novel-length text at low marginal cost could depress markets that human authors depend on, devalue creative labor, and erode the economic viability of writing as a profession. These concerns are not hypothetical. New book releases [tripled between 2022 and 2025](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34777), with new entrants producing predominantly low-quality work, and the publishing industry is already dealing with a surge of undisclosed AI-generated content on major platforms.

This project does not claim to resolve those concerns, and we do not think transparency alone is a sufficient answer to them. What we do believe is that the discussion is better served by inspectable systems than by opaque ones. AI-generated fiction already exists at scale, mostly without disclosure. The research community and the public benefit from being able to examine what these systems produce, how they work, and where they fail, rather than having AI-generated fiction exist only behind closed doors.

This document states norms and expectations, not legal advice. Responsible use depends on the choices of downstream users.
