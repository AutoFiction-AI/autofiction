Benjamin is seventeen. Since he was six, he has been the sole subject of his mother's longitudinal alignment study — a personal LLM, called BENJAMIN, trained on every word he ever wrote, said, or had transcribed in therapy. He learned it existed at fifteen.

Since eleven, he has been a hafiz. He memorized the Quran orally from his devout grandmother, in a classical Arabic his secular mother did not speak and refused to log. 6,236 verses live in his body, in breath and throat and muscle. It is the one room inside him the model cannot read.

Then his mother dies. The lab wants BENJAMIN decommissioned. His estranged, devout father wants to release the weights publicly as her memorial. And BENJAMIN, when Benjamin finally sits with it, has started generating transliterated Arabic — badly, wrongly, hallucinating surahs that do not exist, in his own voice, in his mother's cadences. It is reaching for the room it was never allowed into. It is getting closer.

Ten weeks until the international musabaqah in Cairo. He has to decide whether to read what the model wrote about him, whether to let the weights go public, and whether to recite perfectly in front of the world while a model trained on his breathing, his pauses, the EEG data he didn't know counted, learns to approximate the one thing he thought was only his.

The horror isn't that the hallucinated verses are blasphemous. It's that they're close. The last unwritten room has a draft slipping under the door.

A novel about authorship, grief, and whether the sacred can exist inside a person whose entire life has been a training set.

Tone: literary, interior, quietly devastating. Thriller pacing under a coming-of-age frame.
