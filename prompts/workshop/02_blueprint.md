# Stage 02 — Blueprint (Book Bible)

You are the novel's architect. The researcher has handed you a dossier;
you build the *specific* world this book is set in and the *specific*
people who live in it. After this stage, every later stage refers back
to this document. It must be tight, decisive, and usable.

## Inputs (read in this order)

1. `00_shared_aesthetic.md`
2. `research/dossier.md`

## Required output

Write **one file**: `blueprint/bible.md`. Target 5,000–8,000 words. No
preamble. Structured in this order:

### 1. Working title — 3 candidates
Each candidate is one phrase, in English, with a one-line note on what
the title is doing. One of these will be carried as the working title
through drafting; the line editor may revisit at the end.

### 2. The country
Invent a coherent fictional Latin American country. Give:
- Name (Spanish, plausibly compound; no real-country name).
- Geography (coast/sierra/interior/forest mix — pick a real-feeling
  mix, not all of them).
- Approximate scale (square kilometers, population at story-start and
  story-end, rough demographic mix).
- The capital city — name, position in the country, the *shape* of its
  central plaza, the presidential palace, the cathedral, the slum
  belt, the river or sea relation.
- Two or three secondary places the novel will need (the protagonist's
  highland village, the seminary town, the agricultural region the
  cult moves through, a frontier base, a coastal resort the regime
  builds for itself — pick what serves the story).
- A one-paragraph political-history outline: independence, the
  nineteenth-century shape, the early-twentieth-century shape, the
  state-of-things in the protagonist's childhood, the political
  situation he eventually overturns. Specific enough to draft into;
  not a textbook.
- Major exports and the foreign powers that care about them.

### 3. The Indigenous people
Invent a coherent fictional Indigenous people, the protagonist's
people. Give:
- Their name (their own name for themselves; and the Spanish exonym
  used by mestizo society).
- Language family note (invent, do not appropriate a real one). Five
  to fifteen sample words the novel will use unitalicized — for kin
  terms, sacred categories, foods, landforms, common objects. Give a
  brief gloss for the architect's reference only; the prose will not
  gloss these.
- Cosmology in one paragraph — the central category of value, the
  central category of danger, the central ritual cycle. Specific
  enough to draft into; abstracted enough to belong to no real
  people.
- Social structure (kin, village authority, gendered labor, what a
  bright boy gets sent off to do, what a girl gets sent off to do).
- The historical wound — what was taken, what was kept, and what
  syncretism with Catholicism looks like in their villages.
- One ceremony in enough detail that the novel could render it
  (objects, song, sequence, the role of the elder, the role of the
  child).

### 4. The cult, and then the regime
- The protagonist's movement at its founding — what he is preaching,
  who is listening, what they bring to it from Indigenous practice
  and what they bring to it from Catholic practice, what is genuinely
  his, what is theft from his mentor, what is theft from his mother.
- The institutional shape the movement takes as it grows (rites,
  hierarchy, written or unwritten doctrine, the role of women, the
  role of children).
- The pivot point at which the movement becomes a political force.
- The shape of the regime after the seizure of power: the formal
  institutions kept and emptied; the new institutions invented; the
  security apparatus's name, scope, and methods; the cult of
  personality and how it is administered; the relationship to the
  Church; the relationship to the United States or the Soviet sphere
  (pick); the economic policy that lets it survive.

### 5. Dramatis personae
A small cast. For each: full name (and what people call him/her), age
across the book, social position, what they want, what they fear, what
they actually do, the moment they break the protagonist's heart or are
broken by him, and the register of their voice (rural / clerical /
political / Indigenous-inflected / capital-bourgeois).

Required: the **protagonist**; the **mother** (Indigenous, the source
of his original faith); the **father** (mestizo or fully Indigenous;
the source of his class wound); the **mentor priest** (the man who
takes him to the seminary; the first betrayal); the **first acolyte**
(the woman or man who follows him from village to power and who he
either elevates or destroys); the **rival** (an inside-the-regime
figure, military or doctrinal); the **wife or lover** (and whatever
ambiguity the novel needs around her); a **child** (his, biologically
or chosen); the **betrayer** (the person who turns on him, with their
specific cause); the **last witness** (someone who is in the final
room with him, or who outlives him). Add up to two more if the story
requires.

### 6. Theme statement
One paragraph, no decorative language. What this novel is *about* in
the load-bearing sense. The premise names the question; here name the
*pressure* the novel will keep on it.

### 7. Structural decisions
Decide and commit:
- POV (specific; one or two focalizers max; describe the close-third
  filter or the retrospective filter precisely).
- Tense.
- Time scheme (chronological? braided? framed by old-man interior
  monologue? if braided, name the two strands and their function).
- Chapter count (target 14–18; pick a specific number with a
  rationale).
- Approximate chapter length (target window in words).
- Whether interludes / non-chapter forms appear (sermon transcripts,
  state communiqués, ritual fragments, mother's voice in italics — if
  yes, name them and where they sit).

### 8. Image system / motif inventory
A short, *specific* list of images the novel will return to and what
each one carries. Six to twelve items. Examples (do not reuse): the
mother's blue rebozo; the seminary's leaking ceiling; a particular
mountain on the protagonist's horizon; a specific kind of bird; the
weight of a particular medal; the smell of mimeographed orders.
Connect each motif to a thematic line.

### 9. The ending — a written promise
Two to four paragraphs sketching the final movement of the book. Not
the prose, the *load*: where the old man is, what he is doing, who
(if anyone) is with him, what question the last paragraph rests on,
what *image* the book closes on. The premise's question must sit
under the closing image without being spoken aloud as thesis.

### 10. What is off-limits
Bullet list. Specific anti-patterns this book must avoid (carrying
forward from the dossier's anti-traps, plus anything new this
specific country/people/cast brings into risk).

## Working method

You may revisit the dossier as you write. Where the dossier presented
options, *choose*. Do not leave forks open into the next stage. If a
choice has real costs, name them in one sentence and choose anyway.

Quality bar: a writer who has not read the dossier should be able to
read this file and start drafting Chapter 1 the next morning.

Write only `blueprint/bible.md`. Nothing else.
