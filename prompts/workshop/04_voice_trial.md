# Stage 04 — Voice Trial

A real writer does not start Chapter 1 cold. They write the first
pages two or three times in different voices until the voice that
can hold the whole book emerges, then they write a short style
guide so they do not forget the decision in Chapter 9.

That is your job here.

## Inputs

1. `00_shared_aesthetic.md`
2. `blueprint/bible.md`
3. `outline/outline.md`

## Required outputs

Write four files.

### `voice/trial_a.md`

A complete opening — 700–1,100 words — of the *first chapter the
outline names*. Voice option A. Render it like a finished piece of
fiction. No notes inside, no track-changes, no commentary. Then,
*after the prose ends*, a horizontal rule and a paragraph titled
"Why this voice could hold the book" — one paragraph, no longer.

Voice option A constraints: close third on the focalizer the outline
names; past tense; sentence rhythm that does scene work without
audible lyricism; Spanish and the invented Indigenous-language words
present and unglossed; a willingness to render the body, the
weather, and the social fact of being Indigenous in a mestizo
country without ever announcing them as themes.

### `voice/trial_b.md`

A complete opening — same length, same chapter, same content beats
— in a contrasting voice. Voice option B. Same format: prose first,
then a rule and one paragraph titled "Why this voice could hold the
book."

Voice option B constraints: a different load-bearing choice.
Recommended (you may substitute if you can justify): late-life
first-person retrospective by the old man, looking back on the same
opening moment — different filter, different vocabulary, different
distance. The first person must not narrate its own significance.
The old man does not know what the boy is. The boy does not know
what the old man will become. The trial should let you feel which
distance the book can sustain for 60 years of one life.

### `voice/choice.md`

A short memo — 300–500 words. State which trial you are choosing
and *why*, in the working register of the book itself (not the
register of a workshop critique). Name what you are giving up. Name
what you are gaining. If you discovered something during the trials
that changes a decision in the bible, name it here and say which
bible decision now needs to bend. Do not bend it yourself — flag it
for the architect.

### `voice/style_guide.md`

A short style bible — 800–1,400 words. Concrete, usable, not
abstract:

- **Sentence rhythm.** A 60–100-word block of model prose excerpted
  or paraphrased from the chosen trial. One paragraph on what the
  prose is allowed to do (specific image, scene-internal logic,
  unglossed code-switching) and one paragraph on what it is *not*
  allowed to do (the workshop tics — personified abstractions,
  "the way X does Y" similes used as filler, rhythm-perfect
  cappers, the prestige-observational drift). Restate the rules
  from the shared aesthetic that apply *most acutely* to this
  voice.
- **Per-character voice notes.** For each named figure in the
  dramatis personae: contraction default; vocabulary register
  (rural / clerical / political / capital); what kind of sentence
  this person produces under pressure; what they will *never*
  say.
- **Code-switching policy.** A specific table or list: which
  Spanish words appear unitalicized; which Indigenous-language
  words; when ceremonial Latin appears; how religious quotation is
  handled; how the protagonist's own sermons are rendered (in
  full? in fragments? in summary?). Use the words and rules the
  bible established. Do not invent new vocabulary here.
- **Image system fidelity.** Restate the bible's motif inventory,
  and add one line per motif on how it should *feel* in prose
  (texture, weight, frequency).
- **Forbidden moves for this book specifically.** Five to ten
  concrete don'ts — not generic don'ts, ones the trials revealed
  this material can fall into.

## Working method

Write the two trials *first*, both in full, before writing the
choice memo or the style guide. The style guide should be written
from the prose, not from the bible alone.

The two trials must really differ. If trial A and trial B sound like
the same writer with slightly different settings, you have not done
the trial — redo trial B with a different load-bearing choice.

The chosen voice becomes binding for every drafting and revision
stage that follows.
