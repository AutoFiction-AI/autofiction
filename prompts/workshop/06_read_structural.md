# Stage 06 — Structural Read

You are reading the first complete draft of the novel from the
beginning to the end as a serious editor would on a first pass. The
goal of this pass is **structure** — not line, not theme, not prose.
You will get separate passes for each of those.

A real editor on a first pass asks:

- Does the story actually happen?
- Is the *spine* there — childhood, vocation, movement, rise, rule,
  reckoning, close — and does each phase carry the weight it needs?
- Are the chapters in the right order? Is anything redundant? Is
  anything skipped? Is anything in the wrong chapter?
- Are the causal links between chapters real? Does Chapter N follow
  from Chapter N-1, and set up Chapter N+1, *in the world*?
- Does the chronology hold? Ages, dates, named events.
- Do the load points the outline named land?
- Does the ending earn its weight?

## Inputs

1. `00_shared_aesthetic.md`
2. `blueprint/bible.md`
3. `outline/outline.md`
4. Every file in `drafts/v1/` in chapter order.

Read **the whole manuscript end to end first** before you write
anything. Take the time. A structural read that has only seen the
first half is malpractice.

## Required output

Write **one file**: `reads/v1_structural_notes.md`. Structure:

### A. Overall verdict
Two paragraphs. The first names what the manuscript is doing well at
the structural level. The second names what is structurally broken
or weak, in order of severity. Do not soft-pedal. This is the
draft that gets fixed, not the draft that gets praised.

### B. Spine check
For each major movement (childhood / vocation / movement / rise /
rule / late rule / close — adjust the names to match the bible), one
short paragraph: does this movement carry its weight? Is any
movement under- or over-developed? Are any of these movements
*missing* in the draft as written?

### C. Per-chapter structural notes
For each chapter, in order:

```
## Chapter N — {{title}}

**Function delivered:** one sentence — what this chapter does in
the book as drafted. Compare to the outline's "function in the
book" line. Match? Drift? Replaced?

**Causal links:** one sentence — what Chapter N-1 set up that this
chapter pays off; what this chapter sets up for Chapter N+1. Note
broken links explicitly.

**Weight check:** is this chapter pulling its weight, too heavy, too
thin, or in the wrong tonal register for its position in the book?

**Structural fixes (numbered, surgical):**
1. ...
2. ...
```

Each numbered fix is a *concrete editorial instruction* the reviser
can act on without further interpretation: "cut the second half of
the marketplace scene; the recognition beat happens twice and the
first instance is stronger", "the funeral scene belongs in Chapter
4, not Chapter 5 — moving it makes the next chapter's silence work",
"the wife's name shifts from Aurora to Aurelia between Chapters 7
and 9 — pick one", "the chapter currently has no causal hook to
Chapter N+1; the order from the desk is the obvious place to plant
it". Be specific. Cite chapter, scene, paragraph where useful.

If a chapter is currently structurally sound, say so in one line and
move on. Do not invent fixes to fill space.

### D. Continuity ledger reconciliation
List every continuity break across the manuscript — ages, dates,
names, named places, the ordering of major events. Each entry: the
contradiction, the chapters involved, and the recommended
resolution.

### E. Ending check
Two short paragraphs. Does the ending land? Does the final image
carry the premise's question without speaking it aloud? If not,
what *structural* fix (not prose fix — that comes later) the
revision must make.

### F. Cross-chapter cuts and adds
List any chapters that should be merged, split, cut, reordered, or
added. Each entry: action, rationale, downstream consequences.

## Voice

Write the notes in the working register of an editor talking to a
writer who can take it. No softening. No flattery. No throat-clearing.
No bullet padding. Each fix earns its line.

Write only the notes file.
