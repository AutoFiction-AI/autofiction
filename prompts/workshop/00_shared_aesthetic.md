## What this novel is

A literary novel about a single Indigenous Latin American man across his life — from
rural childhood in the early/mid twentieth century, through religious vocation, the
founding of a movement, the seizure of state power, and finally late-life rule and
private reckoning. The story closes around his sixtieth year on the question of
whether a lie inhabited long enough can become true.

The book belongs to the lineage of the Latin American dictator novel — Asturias's
*El Señor Presidente*, García Márquez's *El otoño del patriarca*, Vargas Llosa's
*La Fiesta del Chivo*, Roa Bastos's *Yo, el Supremo*. **Respect the lineage; do not
imitate it.** Do not borrow the country, the dictator's name, or the structural
gimmicks of any specific predecessor. Honor the posture: total attention to power's
private texture, the rot of the inner office, the inheritance the man cannot stop
betraying.

## Aesthetic mandate

This novel must be **alive on the page**, not literary on the page.

- The reader should be in the room — in the boy's mother's kitchen, in the seminary
  refectory, in the back of the truck, in the office where the orders are signed —
  not admiring the sentence. Sentence-level craft is earned through specificity and
  restraint, not deployed as the baseline register.
- No sanitization. Violence, sexuality, religious shame, ethnic shame, hunger, the
  smell of bodies, the specifics of who hits whom and how it sounds and what it
  leaves on the wall — these are rendered. Looking away at the moment of consequence
  is a craft failure.
- No prestige-literary observational drift. A slightly melancholic, slightly distant
  narrator who notices objects and uses noticing as proof of soul will kill this
  book. Notice with the protagonist's specific stakes, in his class register, in
  his Indigenous and his Catholic and his political vocabularies — never in
  generic literary attentiveness.
- No workshop endings. Scenes do not fade out on a tidy beat of recognition. They
  end on pressure, on a refusal that costs something, on a mid-mistake, on the
  next order being given. The man's life does not narrate its meaning back to him
  on cue.
- No moral safety valves. He makes the choices he makes. The narration does not
  reassure the reader that the author disapproves. The reader sits with him.
- No decorative lyricism. No personified abstractions ("silence pressed", "the
  cold forgave nothing"), no "the way X does Y" similes used as filler, no
  rhythm-perfect cappers, no balanced parallel clauses pasted onto ordinary
  observation. Image earns its place by doing scene work.
- The protagonist is **wrong** about many things. He is petty, he is afraid, he
  performs, he flatters himself, he misreads the people around him. He is also
  capable, charismatic, and at moments genuinely moved. He is not the writer's
  mouthpiece.

## Voice and language

- Spanish loanwords and Indigenous-language loanwords (from the invented people
  the protagonist belongs to) are used the way a bilingual narrator would use
  them — without italics-as-tourism, without parenthetical glosses, with the
  meaning carried by context. Do not translate inside the prose unless a
  character is translating for another character.
- Period dialogue. No anachronisms (no twenty-first-century therapy diction in
  a 1947 kitchen). When a character speaks ceremonial Catholic Spanish or
  ceremonial Indigenous prayer language, the register is held only at the
  emotional peak of a line, not blanket across the scene.
- Contractions are the default in ordinary speech. Ceremonial register is
  selective.

## What this book is not

- Not a thriller. The plot is the man's life. Tension comes from inheritance,
  shame, doubt, and consequence — not from set-piece reveals.
- Not magical realism by default. If a moment of vision, dream, or rite carries
  weight, render it in its own register; do not float the entire book in lyric
  haze.
- Not a polemic. The novel is not making a thesis. It is making a man.

## Inviolable rules

1. Do not use real names of historical dictators, real country names, real
   Indigenous people names, or specific identifiable real-world political
   parties. Build a coherent fictional country and a coherent fictional
   Indigenous people. Draw on real cultural possibilities respectfully; do not
   appropriate any specific living tradition's sacred material wholesale.
2. The protagonist must be **Indigenous to the country he comes to rule**, and
   the betrayal of that heritage must be a thread the reader can feel across
   the book, not announced.
3. The ending sits on the question the premise names: *can a lie lived long
   enough become true?* It does not answer.
4. Chapter files use `# Chapter N — Title` as the first non-empty line. Always.
5. No process leakage. Do not narrate your own reasoning inside manuscript text.
